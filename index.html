About This Research

This work package is part of the ENTRUST Doctoral Network, a Horizon Europe Marie Skłodowska-Curie Actions (MSCA) project bringing together Early Stage Researchers across European institutions to address critical challenges in data governance for agricultural ecosystems.

Agricultural data sharing presents unique governance challenges: farmers lack the infrastructure and legal frameworks to fully exercise data sharing. Our research develops formal, implementable solutions grounded in three complementary and interconnected pillars: sovereignty, trust, and security.

Together, the three pillars form a complete governance layer: data cannot be shared responsibly without enforcing data usage constraints, verifying the trustworthiness of all parties, and securing the channel and content of every exchange.

Pillar 1  ·  Deep Dive
🔒

Data Sovereignty & Usage Control

Formal mechanisms for enforcing data sovereignty rights throughout the agricultural data lifecycle — ensuring that farmers retain meaningful control over how their data is used, shared, and exchanged.

Research Motivation

Agricultural data is generated continuously by precision farming equipment, remote sensors, and third-party platforms. While farmers are legally recognised as data subjects under GDPR, the technical architecture of most agri-data platforms provides no enforceable mechanism for exercising those rights after data is shared. A farmer may consent to data sharing, yet have no way to verify whether their conditions are honoured downstream.

This pillar develops sovereignty constraints, formally specified properties that, once embedded in a data architecture, guarantee sovereignty rights are preserved regardless of how data flows, is transformed, or is re-shared across ecosystem actors.

Pillar 2  ·  Deep Dive
🤝

Mechanisms to Facilitate Trustworthiness of Agricultural Data Sharing

Formal frameworks for evaluating and communicating the trustworthiness of actors and systems in agricultural data sharing networks: enabling evidence-based, informed consent decisions.

Research Motivation

Trust is the precondition for data sharing. Farmers will only share their data with actors they believe to be trustworthy — yet current agricultural data platforms provide no standardised, verifiable way to assess that trustworthiness. Trust is often implicit, contractual, or based on brand reputation rather than verifiable evidence.

The research outlines that farmers do not feel comfortable sharing their agricultural data due to a lack of trust in data sharing processes. Therefore, my research aims to create a mechanism to facilitate the trustworthiness of agricultural data sharing, which aims to overcome farmers' concerns and improve data sharing across the sector. Thus, supporting the advancement of data ecosystems, which may enhance data accuracy and reliability of agricultural data.

Pillar 3  ·  Deep Dive
🛡

Cybersecurity & Risk Assessment

Threat models and security primitives tailored to agricultural data ecosystems — protecting the integrity, confidentiality, and availability of farm data from sensor to analytical platform, in resource-constrained environments.

Research Motivation

Agriculture 4.0 stacks together technologies that were each designed for other contexts: IoT devices from industrial settings, cloud platforms from enterprise IT, wireless communications from telecommunications. The interconnection of these systems creates a distributed attack surface where a compromised soil sensor, a misconfigured API endpoint, or a spoofed identity can cascade through the entire data ecosystem.

Data Governance Frameworks for Agricultural Data Sharing: A Systematic Review

Jakku et al.  ·  NJAS: Impact in Agricultural and Life Sciences  ·  2022

Synthesises 47 studies on agricultural data governance, identifying a persistent gap between legal data rights and technical enforcement capability. Finds that most governance frameworks remain at the policy level without specifying implementable technical mechanisms.

Relevance: Directly motivates our SNF contribution — the gap between governance intent and technical enforcement is precisely what sovereignty invariants address.

An Integrative Model of Organizational Trust

Mayer, Davis & Schoorman  ·  Academy of Management Review  ·  1995

Proposes the seminal three-factor model of trust: a trustee's perceived ability, benevolence, and integrity jointly determine a trustor's willingness to be vulnerable. Distinguishes trust from trustworthiness and models risk as the mediating variable.

Relevance: Theoretical backbone of our trust scoring model; the three antecedents map to verifiable credential attributes in our agricultural VC profiles.

Cybersecurity Challenges in the Agri-Food Sector

Demestichas & Daskalakis  ·  Sustainability  ·  2020

Surveys the attack surface of precision agriculture deployments, cataloguing threats to IoT sensors, SCADA systems, and cloud data pipelines. Applies the STRIDE framework to identify the most prevalent attack categories in agricultural settings, and maps them to countermeasure categories.

Relevance: The closest existing STRIDE application to agricultural IoT; our agricultural threat catalogue builds on and extends this work with data-sharing-specific threat vectors.

Zero Trust Architecture

SP 800-207: Zero Trust Architecture

Rose et al.  ·  NIST  ·  2020

Defines zero trust as a security paradigm that eliminates implicit trust in any network zone, requiring continuous verification for every access request regardless of origin. Specifies the logical components — Policy Decision Point, Policy Enforcement Point, and Policy Information Point — of a zero trust architecture.

Relevance: Foundational to our lightweight zero-trust adaptation; we map NIST's PDP/PEP/PIP architecture onto resource-constrained agricultural edge devices.

EU Cybersecurity Regulation

NIS2 Directive: Measures for a High Common Level of Cybersecurity

European Parliament & Council  ·  EU 2022/2555  ·  2023

The revised Network and Information Security Directive extends mandatory cybersecurity obligations to a broader set of critical sectors, including food and agriculture. Requires entities to implement risk management measures, supply chain security, and incident reporting within 24–72 hours of detection.

Relevance: Regulatory driver for our security requirements matrix; NIS2 obligations create the compliance context in which agricultural cybersecurity artefacts must operate.
